Physical Threshold Selection - Don River

Dylan

2026-03-21

Overview

This notebook documents how the physical flood threshold u_physical was selected for the Don River at Todmorden (02HC024). u_physical is the flow level in m³/s at which water leaves the Don River channel and causes real world damage. It is used to define flood events for the frequency model and as the lower bound for the Bayesian MRL grid search in the next notebook.

The process was not straightforward. The initial approach attempted to use the official TRCA flood warning threshold but was abandoned due to an irreconcilable reference system mismatch between TRCA and Environment Canada data. Three independent empirical approaches were then used to converge on a defensible value. Each approach and its limitations are documented transparently below.

2. Understanding The Water Level Data And The TRCA Problem

The Environment Canada water level data uses a local gauge datum, not elevation above sea level. For 02HC024 Todmorden the normal water level reads approximately 12.1m in the EC data. TRCA’s real-time gauging system reports the same station using metres above sea level (MASL):

trca_levels <- tibble(
  level       = c("Normal", "Low Alarm", "High Alarm", "High-High Alarm"),
  water_level = c(77.59, 78.70, 79.00, 79.30),
  reference   = "TRCA Real-Time Gauging (MASL)"
)
trca_levels
## # A tibble: 4 × 3
##   level           water_level reference                    
##   <chr>                 <dbl> <chr>                        
## 1 Normal                 77.6 TRCA Real-Time Gauging (MASL)
## 2 Low Alarm              78.7 TRCA Real-Time Gauging (MASL)
## 3 High Alarm             79   TRCA Real-Time Gauging (MASL)
## 4 High-High Alarm        79.3 TRCA Real-Time Gauging (MASL)

The High Alarm level of 79.0m MASL was the natural starting point as TRCA defines this as the level at which active flooding occurs. The implied datum offset of 65.4m was estimated by taking the difference between the two normal level readings: TRCA reports 77.59m MASL while Environment Canada records 12.21m for the same normal conditions, giving 77.59 minus 12.21 equals 65.38m. Converting the High Alarm level to local datum then gives approximately 13.6m (79.0 minus 65.4), suggesting 14.0m as a reasonable working estimate for the flood threshold in EC units. However this offset was estimated not verified against an official benchmark elevation document, making 14.0m an approximation rather than an authoritative value.

The water level data covers 2002 onward and has a maximum observed value of 13.54m, which never reached the estimated 14.0m flood threshold. This means the threshold cannot be validated against observed discharge data.

All three approaches below therefore work directly from the discharge record. A future improvement would be to contact TRCA or Environment Canada directly to obtain the official benchmark elevation for 02HC024, which would allow direct conversion and independent validation of u_physical against the official warning threshold.

5. Approach 1: Extrapolation From Rating Curve

rating_model <- lm(log(discharge_m3s) ~ water_level_m,
                   data = rating_curve)

predicted      <- predict(rating_model,
                          newdata = data.frame(water_level_m = 14.0))

(u_extrapolated <- round(exp(predicted), 1))
##       1 
## 27378.9

The log-linear extrapolation to 14.0m produces an estimated discharge of 2.73789^{4} m³/s, a physically impossible result. The Amazon River flows at around 200,000 m³/s and the Don River at its worst recorded event was approximately 400 to 500 m³/s. This confirms that extrapolating 0.5m beyond the observed water level range is unreliable. Hence, approach 1 is a discarded.

The 2013 event records a daily mean of 53.3 m³/s, the highest value for any known flood date in the HYDAT record. The 2017 and 2019 values appear low. This is a consequence of using daily mean discharge.(flood that peaked for 6 to 12 hours gets averaged across the full 24 hour day, significantly understating the true peak flow)

Hurricane Hazel (1954) is absent from the HYDAT comparison because records for 02HC024 only begin in 1961. Hazel is historically documented as the most catastrophic flood event on the Don River with peak flows on the Humber River reaching approximately 1,700 m³/s. Its inclusion here acknowledges the existence of extreme tail events beyond the observed record, directly relevant to the GPD shape parameter discussion in the severity model notebook.

The minimum daily mean across all confirmed flood dates where HYDAT data exists is 2 m³/s and the maximum is 53.3 m³/s. These are daily means, true instantaneous peaks were substantially higher. u_physical should be below this minimum to ensure all genuine flood events are captured while remaining above normal flow conditions.

The 2013 event is classified as primarily pluvial: the discharge recorded at the gauge reflects a combination of surface runoff and riverine flow that cannot be separated at daily resolution. This event is included for completeness and as an upper bound reference but is not used as a primary anchor for u_physical given the fluvial focus of this model. The threshold is derived primarily from the percentile analysis and sensitivity table in Sections 7 and 8.

8. Threshold Decision

Sensitivity Analysis

Event counts were checked across a range of candidate thresholds before committing to 30 m³/s:

sensitivity <- tibble(threshold = c(20, 25, 30, 35, 40)) %>%
  mutate(
    days_above   = map_int(threshold, ~sum(flow_data$flow >= .x,
                                           na.rm = TRUE)),
    pct_above    = round(days_above / nrow(flow_data) * 100, 2),
    rough_events = map_int(threshold, ~{
      flow_data %>%
        mutate(
          above     = flow >= .x,
          new_event = above & !lag(above, default = FALSE)
        ) %>%
        pull(new_event) %>%
        sum(na.rm = TRUE)
    })
  )

A threshold of 30 m³/s sits in the sweet spot: high enough to exclude normal elevated flow, low enough to retain sufficient events for GPD fitting, and consistent with the onset of flood-producing conditions observed in the rating curve and historical event record.

First Decision

U_PHYSICAL <- 30

u_physical = 30 m³/s is selected for the following reasons. It sits above the p99 of 26.1 m³/s meaning flows above 30 m³/s are rare, occurring on only 0.67% of days. It is consistent with observed flood events where all documented events in the rating curve correspond to daily means of 40 to 80 m³/s, making 30 m³/s a conservative lower bound for flood-producing flows. It provides sufficient events for modeling with approximately 40 to 60 independent events expected after proper declustering.

The known limitation is that u_physical is defined in terms of daily mean discharge. True instantaneous flood peaks are higher. This conservative threshold partially compensates by capturing the onset of flood conditions rather than peak conditions.

Note that the event count here is a rough upper bound based on threshold crossings, not proper declustering. The full declustering algorithm using a 3 day minimum separation is implemented in notebook 03_event_extraction.Rmd.

The median event duration of 1 day confirms that the Don River responds and recovers rapidly at 30 m³/s, consistent with a small urban catchment with fast drainage. The minimum inter-event gap of 4 days and zero events separated by fewer than 3 days confirms all 129 events are independent. The 3 day separation criterion was therefore conservative for this dataset as natural event separation was already occurring.

However the 1 day median duration brings a physical concern. A single day spike barely above 30 m³/s is unlikely to represent a damaging flood event. The rating curve analysis in Section 4 showed all documented flood events correspond to daily means of 40 to 80 m³/s. Events between 30 and 40 m³/s likely represent elevated flow conditions rather than active flooding.

Raising u_physical to 40 m³/s produces 54 independent events with a higher mean Q_peak of 53.4 m³/s, better aligned with the documented flood damage range of 40 to 80 m³/s observed in the rating curve. The median event duration remains 1 day across all candidate thresholds, a consistent characteristic of the Don River as a fast responding urban catchment rather than a product of the threshold choice.

54 events sits at the boundary of the practical minimum of 50 exceedances recommended by Coles (2001) for reliable GPD parameter estimation. The Bayesian framework partially relaxes this requirement through prior regularization, but 40 m³/s is the highest threshold that remains statistically workable. Raising to 45 m³/s drops event count to 35, below the minimum regardless of framework.

u_physical is revised from 30 to 40 m³/s on physical grounds. At 30 m³/s the mean Q_peak of 42.2 m³/s and single day median duration suggest many events represent brief elevated flow rather than genuine flooding. At 40 m³/s the mean Q_peak of 53.4 m³/s is consistent with the onset of documented flood damage and the threshold sits above the p99.9 of 52.5 m³/s, capturing only genuinely extreme events.
